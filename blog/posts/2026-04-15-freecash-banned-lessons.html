By Malcolm Lowry X Bluesky · April 15, 2026 · 5 min read

Table of contents

    FreeCash, a reward app with 6 million downloads per month, was pulled Monday following allegations of fraudulent practices in a TechCrunch investigation. The claims: fake reviews, deceptive ads, and potentially illegal data harvesting.

    Update April 22, 2026: FreeCash has denied many of these allegations, though they acknowledged some affiliate ads were misleading. The truth is still emerging. But the industry conversation this sparked matters regardless.

    Moritz Holländer, CEO of Almedia (FreeCash's parent company), posted on LinkedIn about the App Store removal. I posted a supportive reply. Then I read the TechCrunch article detailing the allegations. My jaw dropped.

    I deleted my post - not because I believed every claim, but because the allegations were serious enough to warrant investigation. After the dust settled and FreeCash responded, the picture is more nuanced than the headline suggested.

    As someone who's spent 17 years in mobile gaming user acquisition and online advertising, I've seen most of these tactics employed before - but never all at once, and never this brazenly alleged.

    What Happened: The Allegations

    TechCrunch's investigation alleged FreeCash employed multiple blackhat tactics to reach the top of app store charts. Whether you believe all, some, or none of these claims, here's what was alleged:

    🚩 Allegations from TechCrunch Investigation

    • Alleged data harvesting - TechCrunch claimed FreeCash collected sensitive data (race, religion, sexual orientation, health) without consent. Important context: Reward apps routinely collect demographic data through paid surveys - users knowingly answer these questions for money. The allegation is that FreeCash may have harvested this data beyond the survey context. FreeCash denies this, making me wonder if TechCrunch actually understands how the rewarded space operates. (If they provide evidence FreeCash stole user data, that would change my tune - but they haven't up to this point.)
    • Alleged bot traffic - Claims that install numbers were artificially inflated through non-human traffic. Context: FreeCash's 70M+ users and $1M+/day in payouts suggest real user engagement, though install inflation could theoretically coexist with legitimate usage.
    • Misleading ads (acknowledged) - Some affiliate-run ads claimed users could "earn by scrolling TikTok." FreeCash acknowledged this and stated it came from unauthorized affiliates, not their internal team.

    The Survey Data Question: Standard Practice or Violation?

    Here's where things get murky. In the rewarded app industry, users are paid to answer demographic surveys. This is standard operating procedure. Advertisers want to know who they're reaching. Survey companies (like Nielsen, Cint, Dynata) pay users to answer questions about age, income, shopping habits - yes, sometimes even race, religion, or health status.

    Users opt into these surveys. They're compensated for their data. Everyone in the industry - users, advertisers, platforms - knows this is how it works.

    So when TechCrunch says FreeCash "harvested sensitive data," the critical question is:

    1. Did users answer paid surveys (standard, legal, consensual)?
    2. Or did FreeCash collect this data secretly beyond the survey context?

    TechCrunch implies the latter. FreeCash denies it. Without access to FreeCash's backend, we can't know for certain. But the distinction matters enormously. One is standard industry practice. The other is a massive privacy violation.

    Two possible scenarios:

    • Scenario A (Standard): Users answer paid surveys → survey companies aggregate responses → sell insights to researchers/brands → everyone knows and consents.
    • Scenario B (Alleged Violation): FreeCash collects data beyond surveys → sells raw user profiles to brokers or uses it for their own algorithms → users don't know or consent.

    TechCrunch claims Scenario B. FreeCash denies it. The truth matters.

    Why This Matters for the Industry

    Regardless of what FreeCash did or didn't do, this controversy hurts the entire category.

    When allegations like this surface - even if ultimately disproven - they erode trust. Apple and Google tighten policies. Users become skeptical. Legitimate developers get caught in the crossfire.

    Industry veterans I've spoken with since the ban believe TechCrunch may have misunderstood how survey monetization works. The headline "scammed its way to the top" doesn't match the reality of 70M+ users and $1M+/day in payouts. You can't fake those numbers.

    But perception shapes policy. And right now, the entire category is under scrutiny.

    What We Still Don't Know

    As of this writing (April 22, 2026), key questions remain unanswered:

    • Did Apple/Google ban FreeCash based on TechCrunch's reporting, or independent investigations?
    • Were the alleged "bots" real, or misunderstood organic growth from top-chart positioning and viral affiliate marketing?
    • Did FreeCash knowingly steal or sell user data beyond survey aggregation, or was TechCrunch conflating standard survey practices with illegal harvesting?
    • Will FreeCash return to the app stores after addressing Apple/Google's concerns?

    I don't have answers. Neither does most of the industry. We'll be following closely how this unfolds.

    The Path Forward

    The industry needs this wake-up call - not because FreeCash is guilty (that's still unclear), but because we've been growing too fast without enough transparency.

    Even if every TechCrunch allegation proves false, the fact that they were plausible enough to spark this conversation means we're not communicating clearly enough how this industry works.

    Users deserve to understand:

    • Where their data goes (paid surveys, aggregated insights, anonymized datasets)
    • How payouts work (advertiser pays us, we share with you)
    • What "too good to be true" ads actually mean (spoiler: they're usually affiliate exaggerations)
